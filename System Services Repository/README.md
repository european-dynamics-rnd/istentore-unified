# i-STENTORE Project – System Services Repository

This repository consolidates the flexibility services developed within the i-STENTORE project, with a specific focus on services based on Energy Storage Systems (ESS) and Hybrid Energy Storage Systems (HESS).

The repository provides a structured catalogue of system services, trading services, implementation workflows, semantic data models, and demonstrator-specific instantiations developed during WP3, in particular under Task 3.5: **Implementation of flexibility services based on energy storage technologies**.

The objective is to provide a common and reusable reference for the flexibility services implemented, modelled, harmonised and validated across the i-STENTORE demonstrators.

---

## Purpose of the Repository

The i-STENTORE Service Repository is designed to:

- consolidate the flexibility service catalogue defined during the project;
- describe the role of energy storage systems in providing grid and market services;
- document the implementation logic of each service;
- organise demo-specific service implementations in a consistent structure;
- support interoperability through harmonised data models and semantic alignment;
- provide reusable templates for future replication beyond the project.

The repository follows the evolution of Task 3.5 from the initial service identification phase to the final consolidated service documentation:

1. **Service Definition**  
   Identification and characterisation of flexibility services according to demonstrator requirements and storage technologies.

2. **Service Modelling**  
   Definition of service inputs, outputs, operational constraints, degradation-related parameters and performance indicators.

3. **Semantic Harmonisation**  
   Mapping of service data models to the i-STENTORE ontology and common information models.

4. **API Standardisation**  
   Definition of OpenAPI-compatible and JSON-based interfaces to support interoperability.

5. **Service Validation and Documentation**  
   Consolidation of implementation details, validation outcomes and lessons learned into a structured Service Repository.

## Services and Business Models

Within i-STENTORE, a business model is understood as a conceptual structure describing how a functional application operates, what requirements it has, and how it creates value.

In the ESS domain, this includes:

- the use of storage resources;
- the operational logic needed to provide flexibility;
- the technical and market constraints affecting the service;
- the potential economic value generated by the service;
- the parameters used to determine operational benefits.

The project investigates the interaction between different storage technologies and digital solutions, recognising that energy storage systems are no longer only passive buffering assets. Instead, they can act as active grid components capable of supporting grid resilience, stability, renewable energy integration and efficient system operation.

The i-STENTORE services can be broadly classified into two main families:

### System Services

System services support the secure, reliable and stable operation of the electricity system. These include services such as:

- frequency control;
- congestion management;
- voltage and grid support;
- emergency or resilience-related services;
- power quality and stability support.

### Trading Services

Trading services are linked to market participation and economic optimisation. These include services such as:

- energy arbitrage;
- renewable energy shifting;
- operational optimisation;
- energy market participation;
- capacity or balancing market participation.

The services may operate either:

- **behind-the-meter**, supporting local consumers, industrial sites or energy communities; or
- **front-the-meter**, supporting distribution system operators, transmission system operators or market actors.

The possibility of operating several applications simultaneously is a central aspect of energy storage business models. Service stacking can create additional revenue streams, improve asset utilisation and increase the economic sustainability of storage investments.

## System Services

The services developed within i-STENTORE are generic flexibility and grid-support services applicable to a wide range of storage assets.

Each service may be better suited to a specific storage technology, or to a combination of technologies in the form of a hybrid energy storage system, depending on the power, energy, response time, duration and operational requirements of the service.

The i-STENTORE service catalogue covers different timescales, ranging from sub-second response for frequency support to day-ahead or multi-day optimisation for market and energy-management applications.

---

## Generation Support Services and Bulk Storage

### Energy Arbitrage

Energy arbitrage uses storage to shift energy across time according to market prices or operational value. Energy is stored during low-price or high-renewable-generation periods and discharged during high-price or high-demand periods.

### Peak Shaving

Peak shaving uses energy storage to reduce the maximum power demand observed by a facility, grid connection point or local system.

### Power Smoothing

Power smoothing reduces short-term fluctuations in renewable generation or local power flows.

### Synthetic or Virtual Inertia

Energy storage systems do not provide physical inertia in the traditional sense. However, through advanced power-electronic controls, they can emulate inertia-like behaviour by rapidly injecting or absorbing power in response to frequency deviations.

---

## Services Supporting Distribution Infrastructure

### Congestion Management

Congestion management uses storage to relieve network constraints when power flows approach or exceed the capacity of grid infrastructure. Storage can absorb excess generation when the grid is constrained or inject power locally when demand is high.

---

## Frequency Regulation Services

### Frequency Containment Reserve

Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR) is the first automatic response after a frequency disturbance. The service is activated within seconds and aims to contain the frequency deviation before the system condition deteriorates further.

### Automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve

Automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR) is used after the initial frequency containment phase and aims to restore the system frequency to its nominal value.

### Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve

Manual Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR) is manually activated and typically involves larger and longer-duration adjustments to restore system balance.

### Fast Frequency Response

Fast Frequency Response (FFR) provides very rapid active power injection or absorption to support the grid immediately after a frequency disturbance.

## Interoperability Principles

The i-STENTORE Service Repository follows a service-oriented and technology-agnostic approach. It supports interoperability through harmonised service descriptions, common data structures, semantic alignment with the i-STENTORE ontology, JSON-based data exchange, OpenAPI-compatible interfaces and integration with the i-STENTORE digital ecosystem.
